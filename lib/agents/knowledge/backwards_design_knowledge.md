## Terms

### Understanding by Design (UbD)

A planning framework — *not a philosophy of teaching, not an approach to teaching, a planning framework* (Wiggins, video). Its two key ideas, contained in the title: (1) focus on teaching and assessing for **understanding** and **learning transfer**, and (2) design curriculum **backward** from those ends.

Wiggins's framing in the transcript: *"UbD doesn't help you with [pedagogy / group dynamics], but it does prepare you to think short term, long term, what are we trying to accomplish."* The famous Pasteur quote — *"chance favors the prepared mind"* — captures the discipline: a teacher with a UbD plan is prepared for "teachable moments" not as random serendipity but as fantastic entry points to go where they want to end up.

### The Seven Tenets of UbD (McTighe & Wiggins)

1. **Purposeful curricular planning enhances learning.** UbD helps without offering a rigid process or prescriptive recipe.
2. **Focus on developing and deepening student understanding and transfer of learning** — the ability to effectively use content knowledge and skill.
3. **Understanding is revealed when students autonomously make sense of and transfer their learning through authentic performance.** Six facets — explain, interpret, apply, shift perspective, empathize, self-assess — can serve as indicators of understanding.
4. **Effective curriculum is planned backward from long-term, desired results through a three-stage design process** (Desired Results, Evidence, Learning Plan). Avoids common problems of *treating the textbook as the curriculum* rather than a resource, and *activity-oriented teaching* in which no clear priorities and purposes are apparent.
5. **Teachers are coaches of understanding, not mere purveyors of content knowledge, skill, or activity.** They focus on ensuring learning happens, not just teaching (and assuming that what was taught was learned). They always aim and check for successful meaning making and transfer by the learner.
6. **Regularly reviewing units and curriculum against design standards enhances curricular quality and effectiveness**, and provides engaging and professional discussions.
7. **The UbD framework reflects a continual improvement approach to student achievement and teacher craft.** Results of designs — student performance — inform needed adjustments in curriculum and instruction so that student learning is maximized.

UbD is grounded in two streams of evidence: theoretical research in cognitive psychology, and results of student-achievement studies.

### The Three Stages of Backward Design

**Key concept: alignment** — all three stages must clearly align *not only to standards, but also to one another*. The Stage 1 content and understanding must be what is assessed in Stage 2 and taught in Stage 3.

#### Stage 1 — Identify Desired Results

**Key questions:** *What should students know, understand, and be able to do? What is the ultimate transfer we seek as a result of this unit? What enduring understandings are desired? What essential questions will be explored in-depth and provide focus to all learning?*

In Stage 1, the designer considers goals, examines established content standards (national, state, province, district), and reviews curriculum expectations. Because there is typically more content than can reasonably be addressed within available time, *teachers are obliged to make choices*. Stage 1 calls for clarity about priorities.

**Learning priorities are established by long-term performance goals — what we want students, in the end, to be able to do with what they have learned. The bottom-line goal of education is transfer.** The point of school is not to simply excel in each class, but to be able to use one's learning in other settings.

**Sample transfer goals** (McTighe & Wiggins, Figure 1):
- *Mathematics:* Apply mathematical knowledge, skill, and reasoning to solve real-world problems.
- *Writing:* Effectively write for various audiences to explain (narrative, expository), entertain (creative), persuade (persuasive), and help others perform a task (technical).
- *History:* Apply lessons of the past (historical patterns) to current and future events and issues; critically appraise historical claims.
- *Arts:* Create and perform an original work in a selected medium to express ideas or evoke mood and emotion.

**Sample enduring understandings + essential questions** (Figure 2):

| Enduring Understanding | Essential Question |
|---|---|
| Great literature explores universal themes of human existence and can reveal truths through fiction. | How can stories from other places and times relate to our current lives? |
| Quantitative data can be collected, organized, and displayed in a variety of ways. Mathematical ideas can be represented numerically, graphically, or symbolically. | What's the best way of showing (or representing) ___? In what other way(s) can this be represented? |
| The geography, climate, and natural resources of a region influence the culture, economy, and lifestyle of its inhabitants. | How does where we live influence how we live? |
| The relationship between the arts and culture is mutually dependent; culture affects the arts, and the arts reflect and preserve culture. | In what ways do the arts reflect as well as shape culture? |

**Important Stage 1 point:** Factual knowledge and skills *are not taught for their own sake, but as a means to larger ends*. Acquisition of content is a means, in the service of meaning making and transfer. Teaching equips learners to use or transfer their learning — meaningful performance with content. *This is the result we always want to keep in mind.*

#### Stage 2 — Determine Assessment Evidence

**Key questions:** *How will we know if students have achieved the desired results? What will we accept as evidence of student understanding and their ability to use (transfer) their learning in new situations? How will we evaluate student performance in fair and consistent ways?*

Backward design encourages teachers and curriculum planners to **think like assessors before designing specific units and lessons.** The assessment evidence reflects the desired results identified in Stage 1. *Doing so invariably sharpens and focuses teaching.*

Stage 2 distinguishes between two broad types of assessment:
1. **Performance tasks** — ask students to apply their learning to a new and authentic situation as a means of assessing their understanding and ability to transfer their learning.
2. **Other evidence** — traditional quizzes, tests, observations, work samples to round out the picture.

A key backward-design idea: **alignment**. Are we assessing everything we are trying to achieve in Stage 1, or only the things easiest to test and grade? Is anything important slipping through the cracks because it is not being assessed?

##### The Six Facets of Understanding

When someone *truly understands*, they:

1. **Explain** — concepts, principles, and processes by putting it in their own words, teaching it to others, justifying their answers, and showing their reasoning.
2. **Interpret** — by making sense of data, text, and experience through images, analogies, stories, and models.
3. **Apply** — by effectively using and adapting what they know in new and complex contexts.
4. **Demonstrate perspective** — by seeing the big picture and recognizing different points of view.
5. **Display empathy** — by perceiving sensitively and walking in someone else's shoes.
6. **Have self-knowledge** — by showing meta-cognitive awareness, using productive habits of mind, and reflecting on the meaning of the learning and experience.

**Two cautions when using the facets for assessment:**

1. *All six facets need not be used all of the time in assessment.* In mathematics, application, interpretation, and explanation are most natural; in social studies, empathy and perspective may be added when appropriate.
2. *Performance tasks based on one or more facets are not intended for use in daily lessons.* Rather, they should be seen as **culminating performances for a unit of study.** Daily lessons develop the related knowledge and skills needed for understanding performances, just as practices in athletics prepare teams for the upcoming game.

#### Stage 3 — Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction

**Key questions:** *How will we support learners as they come to understand important ideas and processes? How will we prepare them to autonomously transfer their learning? What enabling knowledge and skills will students need to perform effectively and achieve desired results? What activities, sequence, and resources are best suited to accomplish our goals?*

In Stage 3, teachers plan the most appropriate lessons and learning activities to address **three different types of goals** identified in Stage 1: **transfer, meaning making, and acquisition (T, M, and A)**. McTighe and Wiggins suggest **coding events in the learning plan with the letters T, M, and A** to ensure all three goals are addressed in instruction.

*Too often, teaching focuses primarily on presenting information or modeling basic skills for acquisition without extending the lessons to help students make meaning or transfer the learning.*

Teaching for understanding requires:
- Numerous opportunities to **draw inferences and make generalizations** for themselves (with teacher support).
- Understanding cannot simply be told; the learner must **actively construct meaning** (or misconceptions and forgetfulness ensue).
- Teaching for transfer means learners are given opportunities to **apply their learning to new situations** and receive **timely feedback** on their performance to help them improve.

The teacher's role expands from solely a "sage on the stage" to a **facilitator of meaning making** and a **coach giving feedback and advice** about how to use content effectively.

## Wiggins's "What's the Point of Your Course?" Exercise

From the transcript. Use as a sanity check when auditing.

1. *Think. Pair. Share.* — If you had to write a one-sentence **mission statement** for your course, what would it be?
2. *Apply the backward-design logic:*
   - *If that's the long-term goal, what follows for assessment?*
   - *If that's the long-term goal, what follows for instruction?*
3. *Wiggins's caution:* by *assessing*, he does not mean *grading*. He means *judging how we're doing against the goal* — coaching, giving information about how we're doing against the goal.

**The alignment test (Wiggins's prep-school example):** A school can value "critical and creative thinking" in every program goal statement and mission statement — and a student can still get straight A's at every school in America without it, as long as they are smart, compliant, do their work, and are thorough. *If critical and creative thinking is the goal long-term, then when we go to a plan, we have to make sure week in and week out that we're focusing on critical and creative use of content. Otherwise, we're not going to get it.*

The corollary: *the more you start to think this way, the more you'll realize you're not doing it.*

## Audit Indicators (UbD applied to Canvas)

Flag when reviewing a course in Canvas:

- **Stage 1 missing:** Course landing page, syllabus, or module overviews show topics or readings but no statement of *what students will be able to do* or *what enduring understanding* is intended.
- **Stage 1 → Stage 2 mismatch:** A claimed CLO uses a high-Bloom verb (e.g., *evaluate*, *create*) but every graded assessment uses recall-only or rote-application items.
- **Stage 2 missing performance task:** Every assessment is a quiz/test/observation; no authentic, transfer-style performance task exists at the unit or course capstone level.
- **Stage 3 unbalanced (T-M-A):** Module activities code overwhelmingly as Acquisition (lecture/reading + multiple choice), with no Meaning-Making and no Transfer event.
- **Activity-oriented teaching:** Module structure is "what we will do this week" without traceability to a Stage 1 goal. (Pairs with `designer_thinking_knowledge.md` — design_mode = teacher.)
- **Textbook-as-curriculum:** Course follows textbook chapter order, no explicit Stage 1 goals, no transfer goals stated. (UbD tenet 4 violation.)
- **"Sage on the stage" only:** Instruction is presentation + assignment; no facilitation of meaning-making, no coaching for transfer.

## Quick Reference for Auditors

1. **Stage 1 first.** Does the course state desired results — transfer goals, enduring understandings, essential questions? If absent, the rest of the audit is provisional.
2. **Stage 2 second.** Is there a culminating performance task (not just exams) at unit or course capstone level? Does it require one or more of the six facets?
3. **Stage 3 third.** Code learning-plan events as T / M / A. Is the mix unbalanced toward Acquisition?
4. **Alignment check.** Stage 1 ↔ Stage 2 ↔ Stage 3 — do all three reinforce one another?
5. **Activity-oriented or textbook-driven?** If the course can be described entirely as "we read chapter X and do worksheet Y," tenet 4 is violated.
6. **"Sage on the stage" only?** If no facilitation or coaching events appear, tenet 5 is violated.
7. **Six facets coverage.** Not all six need apply to every course — but if a high-Bloom CLO is claimed and only Explain is probed, flag it.
